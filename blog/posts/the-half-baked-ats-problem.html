The Question No One's Asking Loudly Enough

Was The ATS Ever
Fully Baked - 🥐

When half-cooked AI tools meet AI-generated job descriptions, you do not get innovation. You get a perfectly automated system that widens the gap between those who know how to speak machine and everyone else. Builders, this one's for you.

The oven timer went off. We pulled it out. We called it done. But the centre - Still raw and now it is being served to millions of job seekers every single day.

The ATS problem, in plain bread-and-butter terms

Let's talk about the Applicant Tracking System - that invisible gatekeeper quietly deciding whether your resume even reaches human eyes. It was supposed to make hiring smarter, faster, fairer. Instead, a growing number of professionals, researchers, and frustrated hiring managers are asking a sharper question:

Was the ATS ever actually ready for prime time - Or was it an unfinished product, taken out of the oven too soon, served too hot, and scaled too wide before anyone checked if it actually worked -

The Bread Analogy That Actually Holds

Think about baking a dense sourdough loaf. Pull it out ten minutes early and it looks fine on the outside - golden crust, satisfying thump. But slice it open and you find a gummy, underdeveloped centre. That is not bread. That is a liability wrapped in a convincing exterior.

ATS technology, for many organisations, is that loaf. Developed under commercial pressure, scaled before the edge cases were solved, and now so deeply embedded in hiring infrastructure that questioning it feels almost heretical.

But someone has to ask.

Enter the AI Job Description - And Things Get Worse

Now layer something newer and shinier on top: the AI-generated job description. Companies, under pressure to hire fast and at scale, are using generative AI tools to produce job postings in seconds. Keywords, requirements, buzzwords - all machine-selected, all optimised for what, exactly -

Here is the brutal irony: an AI writes the job description, stuffed with specific keyword patterns. The ATS then filters candidates based on those same keyword patterns. And candidates - the smart ones - are now using their own AI to reverse-engineer the keywords and stuff their resumes accordingly.

⚠ What we have built is a closed loop of machines talking to machines - a game of algorithmic telephone where the actual human being, with their actual skills, actual potential, and actual lived experience, is the one who gets lost in translation.

The job description no longer reflects a real human need written by someone who understands the role. The resume no longer reflects the real person who wrote it. And the filter in between does not understand either. This is not a hiring pipeline. It is a hallucination factory.

  • Use AI to generate a job description loaded with industry buzzwords that sound impressive but reflect no actual operational nuance
  • Feed that description into an ATS that scores candidates on keyword density, not demonstrated competence
  • Watch genuinely skilled candidates - career changers, non-traditional backgrounds, people who speak "human" instead of "LinkedIn SEO" - get auto-rejected
  • Watch less-qualified candidates who know how to game the system sail through to interview stage
  • Complain that "there's no talent in the market" and repeat indefinitely

The Fairness Problem Nobody Wants to Own

ATS systems were also sold, in part, as tools to remove human bias from hiring. Let algorithms decide, the argument went, and you remove the manager who subconsciously favours people who went to the same university. Noble in theory.

In practice - The bias does not disappear - it gets encoded. If the training data or keyword logic reflects historical hiring patterns, which it does, then the algorithm does not remove bias. It launders it. It makes discrimination look like data.

Research has consistently shown that certain groups - people with non-Western names, those with employment gaps, career changers, people with disabilities whose resume formatting differs - are filtered out at disproportionate rates. Not because of their ability. Because of how their story does not fit the template.

So What's the Actual Argument Here -

This is not a call to burn the technology down. ATS tools, in mature, thoughtfully implemented forms, can genuinely assist high-volume hiring. The problem is not the concept. The problem is deployment without accountability.

When AI products - ATS engines, job description generators, resume scoring tools - are released into consequential environments like employment before they have been stress-tested for real-world failure modes, real people pay the price. Not in buggy software. In missed job opportunities. In careers stunted. In brilliant candidates who simply did not know how to speak machine.

The bun was pulled out early. And we are all eating it.

The Digital Divide Nobody Put in the Spec

Here is the part that should make every engineer and product manager uncomfortable: the people most harmed by a half-baked ATS are not randomly distributed. They follow a very predictable map, and that map looks a lot like existing inequality.

ATS optimisation is now a skill. Knowing to use "managed cross-functional teams" instead of "led a group," knowing to mirror the job description's exact phrasing, knowing to use a clean single-column layout that parses cleanly - this is knowledge. And like most knowledge in a digital economy, it is not evenly distributed.

Think about what ATS optimisation actually requires: access to resume review tools, often paywalled, knowledge of how parsing engines work, technical literacy, time to iterate on applications, not available to someone working two jobs, and ideally a network of people who have been through the process before, which means social capital.

The candidate from a well-resourced background with a LinkedIn Premium subscription and a career coach - They know the game. The first-generation graduate in Nairobi, Lagos, or rural India applying for a remote tech role - They are playing a game they do not even know exists, against a system that was never designed with them in mind.

🌍 The cruelest irony: AI hiring tools were explicitly marketed as bias-reduction technology. Instead, they have created a new layer of structural disadvantage - one that is harder to see, harder to challenge, and invisible in the metrics companies use to measure "fairness."

When you ship a product that filters humans at scale and the failure modes fall disproportionately on people who are already marginalised, that is not a bug you can patch in v2. That is a design choice you made by not asking the question in v1.

A Direct Word to the Builders in the Room

If you are an AI developer, an ML engineer, a product manager at an HR tech company, or a founder building the next generation of recruitment tooling, this section is specifically for you.

You are not building neutral infrastructure. Every threshold you set, every feature you weight, every edge case you decide is "out of scope for this sprint" - those are decisions with human consequences. The candidate who does not make it through your pipeline because their resume came from a free template with two columns - That is a real person. The system did not fail them neutrally. The system failed them in the exact direction you did not test for.

The questions worth asking before you ship are not just "does it work - " They are: Who does it work for - Who does it fail - And are the people it fails already disadvantaged - If you cannot answer those three questions with data before deployment, the product is not ready.

That is not idealism. That is engineering rigour applied to the full problem space, not just the comfortable parts.

Let's put some real questions on the table 👇

  • - If your ATS disproportionately filters out candidates from low-income countries, non-Western educational backgrounds, or mobile-only applicants, is that a bug, or a feature you never bothered to check for -
  • - If an AI writes the job description and an AI filters the applications, who is actually responsible when the wrong person gets hired, or the right person gets missed -
  • - Should companies be legally required to disclose when an ATS or AI tool was used in any stage of candidate filtering -
  • - Is "ATS optimisation" now a prerequisite skill for employment, and what does it say about us that we accept this -
  • - Are we heading toward a future where the best candidates are simply the best prompt engineers -
  • - At what point does deploying an insufficiently tested AI tool in a high-stakes domain, like hiring, become an ethical violation, not just a product shortcoming -

The hiring industry is at an inflection point. Organisations that figure out how to use AI as a complement to human judgment, rather than a replacement for it, will build better teams. Those who keep serving half-baked loaves and wondering why their talent pipeline tastes wrong - They will keep having the same conversation in 2030 that they are having today.

The oven is a tool. Knowing when the bread is actually ready - that is still a human job.

The Take

AI in hiring is not inherently broken - but releasing AI tools into consequential human systems before they are genuinely ready is. The ATS, as widely deployed today, is a case study in what happens when we optimise for speed-to-market over fitness for purpose. Pair it with an AI job description, and you have a self-sealing system that is excellent at one thing: finding candidates who are good at gaming systems, not necessarily good at the job.
